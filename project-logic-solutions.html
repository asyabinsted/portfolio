Project duration: Mar'24-Sep'25

Team: 1 Product owner, 1 Project manager, 2 FE developers, 4 BE developers

Challenge & Objective

The platform in use at the time had started as an accounting tool and gradually grew to support multiple departments. Over time, it became powerful and feature-rich, but also complex and hard to use.

While it worked reliably and offered many functions, it had major limitations. It was built in the early 2000s on technology that made changes difficult and restricted design improvements. Navigation was confusing, workflows were manual, tasks had to be repeated, and information was scattered. New users required special training that took a long time to understand how everything worked, and tracking shipments was slow and error-prone.

My task was to design a platform for all agents in the logistics process, with the goal of easing onboarding on a complex system. The aim was to help new users get started quickly, understand workflows easily, reduce repeated work, and make information simple to find and manage.

Design Process

To understand the challenges of freight forwarding, I first studied the rules and regulations involved in moving goods across countries. I analyzed other systems to see how similar workflows were handled and talked with users to learn how tasks were actually performed.

I decoded data from other platforms to ensure the design would work with existing systems and information flows. I created low-fidelity wireframes to explore ideas and test them early with users. Later, I developed hi-fidelity interactive prototypes to simulate real interactions and gather feedback on usability before development.

I conducted user interviews, mapped workflows, ran surveys to collect feedback, and tested different solutions. These steps helped shape a platform where workflows matched how people actually worked, tasks were automated or streamlined, information was centralized so work was less fragmented, and different agents could access the shipment information relevant to them.

Key Screens

The dashboard, overview, and tracking screens are central to how people interact with the platform. They are the starting points for daily work, for checking progress, and for following shipments. These screens were also used by external stakeholders such as customers and other agents in the logistics chain. Making them clear and accessible was important to open the platform beyond internal teams and support collaboration across different roles.

Shipment Creation Flow

PROBLEM

Creating a shipment was fragmented and repetitive. Sales, operations, and other agents entered the same information in different systems, which increased errors and slowed work. Each user had their own way of handling the process, making training and onboarding long and inconsistent.

DESIGN

I created a single flow where any authorized user could start a shipment â€” from sales, customer request, or operations â€” all leading to the same shared record. To reduce errors and save time, the flow:

  • Starts with only a few required parameters (operation type, transport mode, service type) to generate a shipment reference.
  • Guides users step by step through a "journey," replacing manual workflows with clear pages grouped by logic (basic info, owners, stakeholders, shipment details, locations, services).
  • Allows reusing previous shipments as templates instead of starting from scratch.
  • Supports automation: repeated fields are auto-filled, detailed packing lists can be uploaded and parsed, and calculations (like container volume) are done automatically.
  • Provides an AI assistant that retrieves data from emails and documents, highlights conflicts, and suggests missing information â€” while still leaving users in control to review and confirm.

OUTCOME

This flow reduced duplicate work and made shipment creation more consistent. Users could start from the same source of truth instead of re-entering data in different systems. Information stayed connected across departments, which shortened onboarding and reduced mistakes. â†“

Consistency & Design System

The platform's interface was built on the Shadcn component library, providing a solid foundation for consistent patterns and interactions. Components were customized to fit the specific workflows and needs of different users.

I developed a color and semantic token system and implemented light and dark modes, ensuring a cohesive look and behavior across all screens. This approach improved clarity, predictability, and usability, helping users navigate the platform more efficiently and shortening onboarding time.

Consistency in components, patterns, and interactions was applied across the platform, creating a more predictable and intuitive experience for both internal users and external stakeholders.

Takeaways

  • Complexity can be explained simply: freight forwarding is full of regulations, handovers, and data exchanges. Breaking it into clear flows helped me see how design can organize complicated systems.
  • Onboarding is design work: I realized that many of the frustrations users faced weren't about logistics itself but about how hard it was to learn the tool. Reducing training time by making flows clearer was one of the most valuable design outcomes.
  • Flexibility needs boundaries: users in logistics often have different habits and priorities, so the system had to adapt. But unlimited freedom quickly becomes chaos. I learned how to design options and customizations while still keeping the product coherent.
  • Design lives between people and systems: talking to users gave me one view of their struggles, but looking at how systems communicated with each other showed another. Bridging both sides was key to making the product useful.
